## Context
These are excerpts from user interviews conducted with pregnant women diagnosed with GDM, healthcare providers, and family members in Kerala, India. Read through all interviews and identify patterns to build goal-directed personas.

## Interview 1: Priya M.
**Age:** 28  
**Occupation:** Software Engineer  
**Location:** Kochi (Urban)

### Background
- First pregnancy, 24 weeks  
- Diagnosed with GDM at 20 weeks  
- Lives with husband in apartment, both work full-time  
- Speaks English and Malayalam  

### Key Quotes
> "I check my glucose 4 times a day but writing everything in the paper logbook is tedious. I use my phone for everything else - why not this?"

> "The clinic is 45 minutes away. I take half a day off work for a 10-minute appointment. Doctor just asks if I'm tracking and tells me to 'eat healthy.'"

> "I want to know what's safe to eat. Google gives me information about Western diets - bread, pasta. We eat rice, dosa, idli. What's my carb count for upma?"

> "My husband wants to help but doesn't understand the diet rules. Sometimes he brings home sweets thinking fruit is always okay."

### Pain Points
- Manual logging is time-consuming  
- Limited time with doctor (5-10 minutes)  
- Lack of Kerala-specific dietary guidance  
- Family doesn't understand GDM management  

### Technology Use
- Uses smartphone (Android) daily - WhatsApp, Instagram, banking apps  
- Comfortable with app-based services  
- Prefers English for medical terms, Malayalam for casual use

## Interview 2: Lakshmi R.
**Age:** 32  
**Occupation:** Homemaker  
**Location:** Wayanad District (Rural)

### Background
- Third pregnancy, 28 weeks  
- Diagnosed with GDM at 24 weeks  
- Lives in joint family (mother-in-law, sister-in-law)  
- No formal employment outside home  
- Speaks Malayalam only  

### Key Quotes
> "The government hospital is 2 hours by bus. I go once a month. They gave me a machine to check blood sugar but nobody explained how it works properly."

> "I can't read English. The diabetes booklet they gave me is all in English. ASHA sister tries to explain but she's busy with many families."

> "My mother-in-law cooks. She thinks I'm being difficult when I ask for different food. She says she ate normal food during her pregnancies and was fine."

> "I don't know how to use smartphone very well. My husband has one but I mostly just answer calls. Typing is hard for me."

### Pain Points
- Geographic isolation from healthcare (2-hour travel)  
- Language barrier (materials all in English)  
- Limited digital literacy (can't type well)  
- Family pressure to eat traditional diet  
- Difficulty operating glucose meter  

### Technology Use
- Basic phone user (mostly calls, husband has smartphone)  
- Cannot type well, prefers voice/calls  
- ASHA worker visits once a week  
- No reliable internet at home

## Interview 3: Dr. Sarah Thomas
**Role:** OB/GYN  
**Location:** Government Hospital, Kochi

### Background
- 12 years experience  
- Sees 50-60 patients per day in public hospital  
- Manages 30-40 GDM patients at any time  
- Also runs private clinic 2 evenings per week  

### Key Quotes
> "I have 5-10 minutes per patient. I can't review their entire glucose log. I need to see trends, patterns, red flags quickly."

> "Many patients don't bring their logbook or it's incomplete. They say they forgot or lost the paper. Then I'm making decisions without data."

> "I refer high-risk patients to the endocrinologist but the waiting time is 6-8 weeks. I need to manage them in the meantime without specialist backup."

> "Cultural diet counseling takes time I don't have. Dietitian sees patients once, maybe twice total. Patients need ongoing support."

> "When patients don't follow up, I have no way to reach them. By the time they return, sometimes the pregnancy is high-risk."

### Pain Points
- Severe time constraints (5-10 min per patient)  
- Incomplete or missing patient data  
- Limited specialist support available  
- Cannot monitor patient compliance between visits  
- No proactive outreach system for non-compliant patients  

### Technology Use
- Uses basic hospital EMR system  
- Email and WhatsApp for urgent patient communication  
- Would use a provider dashboard if it existed  
- Needs mobile access (checks patient info between appointments)

## Interview 4: Meera S.
**Role:** ASHA Worker  
**Location:** Kollam District (Rural)

### Background
- 8 years as ASHA (Accredited Social Health Activist) worker  
- Covers 15-20 households in rural area  
- Conducts home visits, health education  
- Refers patients to Primary Health Center (PHC)  
- Completed 10th standard education  

### Key Quotes
> "I visit Lakshmi every week. She has the glucose machine but doesn't check regularly. She says it hurts to prick her finger."

> "I try to explain the diet but I'm not expert. I tell them what doctor said but they have questions I can't answer."

> "Some families think GDM is not serious. They say 'it's just pregnancy, it will go away after delivery.' They don't understand the baby is at risk."

> "I have 20 pregnant women in my area. I can't remember who checked glucose when, who has appointment coming up. I write in notebook but it's messy."

> "If I had a way to see all my patients' data in one place, I could remind them. But I can't call everyone every day."

### Pain Points
- Limited medical knowledge for patient questions  
- Cannot track all patients effectively (paper notebook)  
- No reminder system for patient compliance  
- Difficulty motivating patients who don't see symptoms  
- Communication gap between PHC doctors and patients  

### Technology Use
- Has smartphone (government-provided)  
- Uses WhatsApp for coordination with other ASHAs  
- Basic app literacy, prefers simple interfaces  
- Needs offline functionality (network spotty in rural areas)

## Interview 5: Ravi M.
**Role:** Husband of Priya  
**Age:** 30  
**Occupation:** IT Sector  
**Location:** Kochi

### Background
- Wife Priya has GDM (first pregnancy)  
- Works in IT, comfortable with technology  
- Wants to support wife but unsure how  
- Attends some doctor appointments when schedule permits  

### Key Quotes
> "I want to help but I don't understand what foods are okay. Yesterday I bought mangoes thinking fruit is healthy. Priya said it has too much sugar."

> "When she's tired or stressed, she skips glucose checks. I try to remind her but then I feel like I'm nagging."

> "I read online that GDM can cause complications. I'm worried but don't want to stress her out more by constantly asking about her numbers."

> "Sometimes she gets low blood sugar at night. I don't know what to do. Should I call the doctor? Give her juice? I feel helpless."

### Pain Points
- Lacks knowledge about GDM management  
- Wants to support without being overbearing  
- Doesn't know emergency protocols (hypoglycemia)  
- Can't access wife's glucose data to understand patterns  
- No direct communication channel with healthcare provider  

### Technology Use
- Smartphone user (Android)  
- Would use an app if it helped him support his wife  
- Interested in educational content about GDM  
- Wants to be notified of concerning readings
